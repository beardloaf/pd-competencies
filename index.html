How to use this matrix

For hiring, growth conversations, and team planning.

In practice

Three ways to use it

For hiring

  • Pick the role level. Senior, Staff, Principal, or Distinguished?
  • Check for 60%. Hold their work up against the level below.
  • Look for next-level signs. Are they ready to step up? That's the 60% check.

For growth

  • Baseline. Which level have they reached today?
  • Growth edge. What's 60% of the next level for them?
  • Plan. Spend the next 6–12 months closing that gap.

For team planning

  • Find the gaps. e.g. "No one's at Principal level for Product Thinking."
  • Spot weak spots. Which competencies are thin across the team?
  • Hire to fill. e.g. "We need Staff-level Tooling & Automation."
Philosophy

The principles behind the model

01

The 60% rule

No one needs to be perfect at their current level — they need to show they're ready for the next one. That usually means:

  • Doing next-level work in small doses
  • Teaching others at their own level
  • Spotting next-level problems and openings
  • Asking sharper questions than their peers
02

Growth is uneven

People don't move up evenly across the five. Someone can be Staff in Execution but Principal in Product Thinking. That's fine — use the matrix to see where they're strong and where to push:

  • Score each competency on its own
  • Expect spikes — strong here, lighter there
  • Weigh the whole shape, not one high mark
  • Aim coaching at the low spots
03

Levels stack

Staff includes everything from Senior, plus more. It's not different work — it's bigger scope and more ownership:

  • Every level keeps the ones below it
  • Up means wider scope, not different tasks
  • More ownership, more ambiguity
  • Senior habits still matter at Principal

Org Design

Structure around judgment, not project teams. Each cell is a person, their area, and the number of engineering teams they cover (or designers, for leaders).

How to use this matrix

For hiring, growth conversations, and team planning.

In practice

Three ways to use it

For hiring

  • Pick the role level. Senior, Staff, Principal, or Distinguished?
  • Check for 60%. Hold their work up against the level below.
  • Look for next-level signs. Are they ready to step up? That's the 60% check.

For growth

  • Baseline. Which level have they reached today?
  • Growth edge. What's 60% of the next level for them?
  • Plan. Spend the next 6–12 months closing that gap.

For team planning

  • Find the gaps. e.g. "No one's at Principal level for Product Thinking."
  • Spot weak spots. Which competencies are thin across the team?
  • Hire to fill. e.g. "We need Staff-level Tooling & Automation."
Philosophy

The principles behind the model

01

The 60% rule

No one needs to be perfect at their current level — they need to show they're ready for the next one. That usually means:

  • Doing next-level work in small doses
  • Teaching others at their own level
  • Spotting next-level problems and openings
  • Asking sharper questions than their peers
02

Growth is uneven

People don't move up evenly across the five. Someone can be Staff in Execution but Principal in Product Thinking. That's fine — use the matrix to see where they're strong and where to push:

  • Score each competency on its own
  • Expect spikes — strong here, lighter there
  • Weigh the whole shape, not one high mark
  • Aim coaching at the low spots
03

Levels stack

Staff includes everything from Senior, plus more. It's not different work — it's bigger scope and more ownership:

  • Every level keeps the ones below it
  • Up means wider scope, not different tasks
  • More ownership, more ambiguity
  • Senior habits still matter at Principal

Design Team

Create & Capture Demand

Two pillars, both reporting to the CEO. Create makes demand. Capture takes it. Content, data storytelling, and AI production are disciplines inside Create — not the name of the pillar itself.

Create
Brand · creates demand

Owns awareness, share of voice, branded search, and earned reach. Narrative, identity, AI production, and comms all sit here as one connected engine.

  1. Narrative & content engine
    The story, produced at volume by AI, across as many formats as it needs. A human owns the angle and whether it's true — not the drafting.
  2. Data storytelling new
    Turns the company's own data into narrative — proprietary reports, benchmark studies, state-of-the-industry pieces. Works off the same numbers Capture's backbone tracks, told as a story instead of a dashboard.
  3. AI production new
    Generative video, imagery, and asset production at volume. Feeds Create's own campaigns and Capture's ad and page templates from one place, so quality doesn't fork.
  4. Identity & taste
    The standard everything above is judged against, human- or AI-made. The scarcest job in the org once agents can produce infinite variations.
  5. Comms & reputation
    Press, analyst relations, internal. Earned trust — a different job than manufactured reach.
Capture
Growth · captures demand

Owns pipeline, CAC, conversion, ROAS, and NRR. Centralized crafts — demand, lifecycle, analytics, product marketing, and events — all serving one growth motion. Not a product org.

  1. Demand generation
    Paid, SEO, AEO, web, and lead routing. The core motion behind pipeline, CAC, and ROAS.
  2. Product marketing
    Positioning, messaging, launches, competitive intel, and sales enablement — centralized, serving every audience.
  3. Lifecycle marketing
    Retention, expansion, onboarding comms. Where NRR actually gets managed day to day.
  4. Analytics & attribution
    Pipeline attribution and the shared systems and agents both pillars run on. Sits here for management; roadmap co-owned with Create.
  5. Events
    Which events to attend, booths, logistics, on-site. Flagship brand moments need Create sign-off on concept.

The AI & analytics backbone sits inside Capture for management, but its roadmap is co-owned with Create — neither pillar can quietly redirect it.

All brands are media companies now.

Create content. Maintain and gain relevance.

Attention is the scarce resource, and brands are earning it like media companies instead of renting it like advertisers — publishing consistently, owning audiences, treating content as infrastructure.

The money already agrees: content pulls 26% of marketing budgets and $100B+ globally, and it's the ad spend that gets cut first when things tighten.

Content marketing is a $100B+ market.

The category's global revenue is projected to clear $100 billion in 2026, on its way to $1.95 trillion by 2032.

Budgets have shifted from campaigns to editorial ops.

Content now claims 26% of total marketing spend, with 42.7% of teams putting a fifth to a third of their budget into creative production alone.

Documented content strategy has become table stakes.

73% of B2B and 70% of B2C marketers now run one, nearly double the ~40% who did in 2018.

Creator distribution is now a real budget line, not an experiment.

Influencer marketing spend crossed $10 billion in 2025 and is growing another 15.7% this year.

Attention itself is the scarce resource.

US consumers now spend 7+ hours a day in digital media, which is exactly why one-off ad campaigns can't move the needle like they used to.

AI is our derivative-content pipeline.

The role of AI creative producer sets the prompts, formats, and quality bar for every cut — turning one anchor into a dozen tellings without watering it down.

Why this change matters

What the shape enforces.

Create is the umbrella; content is a discipline inside it.

Content is what gets made. Create is what it's made in service of — positioning, reputation, identity. Naming the pillar for the output puts production above the thing it's supposed to serve.

Agents produce. People own judgment.

Data storytelling and AI production sit here explicitly because they're where this shows up hardest — huge volume of video, imagery, and data-driven content still needs one person deciding what's true and what's good.

The shared backbone gets a governance rule, not a new box.

The AI & analytics backbone sits inside Capture day to day — no new seat, no new reporting line. But its roadmap is co-owned with Create in writing, so it can't quietly become a Capture-only tool.

Create sets the standard once; Capture executes inside it at speed.

Most work ships with no gate — only new-reach, new-audience, or story-changing work gets reviewed. Rules of engagement are the road-tested part; keep them.

PLG belongs to Product; investor narrative to Finance.

Conversion, activation, and in-product upsell are a product decision. Investor and corporate narrative pulls tone from Create but sits with CEO/Finance — that audience needs financial fluency, not brand voice.

AI-native designers go deeper, not wider.

Tools absorb skills, not judgement. We optimize for judgement because that's what sets designers apart.

AI doesn't turn designers into ground cover. It's the opposite.

As tools take over the busywork, designers move deeper—into the framing, the logic, the build that used to belong to someone else.

Collaboration is down 20% in 2026 vs. 5% in 2025. There's more solo / prompting time than collab time.

Engine wants more control over the user experience, not less.

The more they absorb, the more SME each area demands—fewer areas held well beats more areas held thin.

Designers are now running 2x as many AI tools as they were in 2025.

Designers (65%) pick up more xfn work than PMs and EMs (40%).

Absorbing roles isn't one-directional, but it's not equal either. Designers absorb PM and EM work faster and it's been trending this way since before AI.

New hybrid titles (designer engineer, builder) are showing up across job boards.

The driver and their intuition matter more than the car you put them in.

Same tools. Same access. Different judgment and intuition. EM: code. PD: experience. PM: business. Hard skills are easier to pick up than soft ones.

Hiring managers still want AI fluency, but hold an equally high bar for craft, vision, and judgment.

Boundaries are dissolving from both sides, not just design expanding.

Fewer people, holding deeper ground, not more territory. Coordination is shifting to tools, not people.

Only 28% of companies have updated hiring or performance practices to match. Just 4% have updated comp.
